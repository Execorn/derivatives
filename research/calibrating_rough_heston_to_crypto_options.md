Advanced Calibration of the Rough Heston Model to Cryptocurrency Options Microstructure1. Introduction to Cryptocurrency Derivatives and Rough VolatilityThe maturation of cryptocurrency derivatives markets has necessitated the application of advanced stochastic volatility models to accurately capture the complex dynamics of digital assets. Options on Bitcoin (BTC) and Ethereum (ETH) exhibit unique market microstructures, extreme volatility levels, and pronounced right-skewed volatility smiles that challenge traditional pricing frameworks. The classical Heston model, which assumes that the instantaneous variance process is driven by a standard Brownian motion, frequently fails to capture the explosive nature of the at-the-money (ATM) volatility skew observed at very short maturities. Consequently, the quantitative finance community has increasingly turned toward rough volatility frameworks.The Rough Heston model, characterized by a fractional Brownian motion driver with a Hurst exponent $H < 0.5$, has emerged as a superior framework for capturing the explosive short-dated implied volatility skew and the rough, anti-persistent nature of realized volatility observed in both equity and cryptocurrency markets. Unlike traditional models where the volatility paths are relatively smooth, rough volatility models assert that the volatility process is Hölder continuous of order $H-\epsilon$, making the paths highly irregular.Calibrating a Rough Heston model equipped with a learnable Hurst exponent to cryptocurrency options presents an intersection of quantitative finance, high-frequency data engineering, and deep learning. The process requires a rigorous understanding of inverse option payoffs, risk-neutral forward curve extraction via put-call parity, and advanced numerical methods to overcome the instabilities of fractional Riccati equations. Furthermore, the deployment of Fourier Neural Operators (FNO) for deep calibration demands a radical re-parameterization of the training space to accommodate the exceptionally high variance and rapid regime shifts inherent to digital assets.This analysis provides an exhaustive framework for executing this calibration pipeline, bridging the theoretical mechanics of rough volatility with the programmatic realities of the Deribit exchange infrastructure. The framework sequentially addresses data acquisition, microstructural adjustments, theoretical pricing dynamics, and the implementation of machine-learning-accelerated calibration algorithms.2. Microstructural Idiosyncrasies of Cryptocurrency OptionsThe microstructure of cryptocurrency options fundamentally diverges from traditional equity and commodity options. Attempting to calibrate a Rough Heston model without adjusting for these microstructural idiosyncrasies will yield theoretically invalid parameters and catastrophic hedging errors.3.1 Inverse Options and Payout MechanicsThe vast majority of BTC and ETH options traded on Deribit are engineered as inverse options. In traditional finance, options are typically linear: the strike price and the underlying asset are quoted in fiat currency, and the settlement occurs in fiat. On Deribit, while the strike price $K$ and the underlying index $S_T$ are quoted in USD, the premium, the margin requirements, and the final settlement are denominated and paid strictly in the underlying cryptocurrency itself (BTC or ETH).For a standard European call option on equities, the payoff at maturity $T$ is calculated as $\max(S_T - K, 0)$ in fiat currency. However, for an inverse crypto option, the payoff must be divided by the settlement price to convert the fiat value back into the cryptocurrency token. The payoff functions, denominated in coins, are calculated as follows :$$\Pi_{call} = \frac{\max(S_T - K, 0)}{S_T} = \max\left(1 - \frac{K}{S_T}, 0\right)$$$$\Pi_{put} = \frac{\max(K - S_T, 0)}{S_T} = \max\left(\frac{K}{S_T} - 1, 0\right)$$This non-linear payout structure implies that an inverse call option is mathematically equivalent to a standard put option on the inverse exchange rate (e.g., the USD/BTC rate instead of the BTC/USD rate), effectively representing a put option on the inverse price $1/S_T$ with a reciprocal strike of $1/K$. Consequently, the traditional Black-Scholes Greeks must be entirely reformulated. As the spot price of Bitcoin increases, the fiat value of the call option's payoff increases; however, the purchasing power of the Bitcoin in which the payoff is denominated also increases. This creates a distinct concavity in the fiat-equivalent payoff profile that alters the delta and gamma hedging dynamics for market makers.3.2 Mark Price, Last Trade Price, and Implied Volatility InterpolationWhen extracting the implied volatility surface from the exchange for calibration purposes, selecting the correct price anchor is critical. Calibrating against the last_trade_price is universally recognized as flawed in crypto markets. Due to the extreme dispersion of strikes and the illiquidity of certain deep out-of-the-money (OTM) options, the last trade price may be stale by several hours, reflecting a completely different volatility regime.Calibration must rely on either the bid-ask midpoint or the exchange's proprietary mark_iv. Deribit calculates the mark price, and consequently the mark_iv, using a continuous, real-time proprietary Black-Scholes solver applied to the live order book. This solver filters out outlier quotes and creates a smoothed volatility surface that dictates the margin requirements for all traders.While utilizing mark_iv provides a clean, pre-smoothed dataset, quantitative researchers focused on model-free calibration often prefer parsing the raw order book to construct their own mid-prices. This requires rigorous filtering protocols: options with zero open interest or bid-ask spreads exceeding a predetermined threshold (e.g., 20% of the bid price) must be aggressively pruned to prevent the FNO from fitting to microstructural noise.3.3 Expiries, Liquidity Concentration, and 24/7 Trading DynamicsUnlike traditional exchanges that operate with defined trading hours and standardized monthly expirations, cryptocurrency markets trade 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, 365 days a year. Deribit lists a continuous spectrum of expiries, including daily (often referred to as 0DTE or 1DTE depending on the time of day), weekly, monthly, and quarterly contracts.All options on Deribit exhibit European exercise styles and are cash-settled against the Deribit Index at exactly 08:00:00 UTC on the day of expiration. The 24/7 nature of the market alters the mechanics of theta decay. In traditional equity markets, theta decay over the weekend is often priced into the Friday close. In crypto, weekend trading is highly active, though liquidity profiles change. Volume and open interest tend to concentrate heavily in the weekly (Friday) and quarterly expirations, while daily expiries serve as vehicles for pure event-driven speculation. Gaps in the option chain are common, particularly in the deep OTM strikes for longer-dated maturities, necessitating interpolation grids before calibration.3.4 The Economics of the "Crypto Skew"In traditional equity index options, such as the S&P 500, the implied volatility surface typically exhibits a pronounced left-skew, meaning that out-of-the-money put options trade at a significant premium to equidistant call options. This equity skew is driven by the leverage effect (where dropping equity prices mechanically increase corporate leverage and risk) and the structural institutional demand for portfolio insurance against market crashes.Cryptocurrency options, conversely, frequently exhibit a persistent right-skew, commonly referred to as the "crypto smile," which heavily favors upside calls. BTC options consistently demonstrate that OTM calls are more expensive than OTM puts in terms of implied volatility.This distinct phenomenon is economically driven by the market's demographic composition and structural yield mechanisms. The crypto market is heavily skewed toward retail and crypto-native institutional demand for leveraged upside exposure. Traders continuously purchase deep OTM calls during bull market phases to capture asymmetric upside. Furthermore, there is a distinct lack of natural long-side asset holders willing to systematically overwrite calls (execute covered call strategies) at a scale sufficient to depress call premiums. In traditional finance, large asset managers write calls for yield; in the cryptocurrency ecosystem, institutional holders typically generate yield through on-chain staking protocols or basis trading, removing the natural supply of call overwriting. Consequently, the positive correlation between spot price movements and realized volatility in crypto markets structurally elevates the cost of OTM calls, a feature that the Rough Heston model must perfectly encapsulate through its correlation parameter ($\rho$).4. Risk-Free Rates, Implied Forwards, and Put-Call ParityThe theoretical foundations of option pricing require the determination of a risk-free rate and the forward price of the underlying asset. Cryptocurrencies possess no intrinsic dividend yield, but they carry unique costs of carry driven by institutional borrowing constraints and the yield available in decentralized finance or perpetual futures markets.4.1 The Divergence of Crypto Risk-Free Rates from Fiat BenchmarksBecause the crypto risk-free rate differs fundamentally from traditional fiat risk-free rates, utilizing standard benchmarks like the Secured Overnight Financing Rate (SOFR) or US Treasury yields is theoretically incorrect for pricing inverse crypto derivatives. If a trader were to use fiat rates, they would be ignoring the credit risk inherent to the cryptocurrency exchange and the lack of a frictionless fiat-to-crypto arbitrage bridge.4.2 Perpetual Funding Rates as a Proxy for CarryThe most liquid derivatives in the cryptocurrency ecosystem are perpetual swaps, which are futures contracts without an expiration date. To prevent the price of the perpetual swap from deviating from the spot index, exchanges utilize a funding rate mechanism. Every eight hours, if the perpetual contract trades at a premium to the spot price, long position holders pay a funding fee to short holders. If it trades at a discount, shorts pay longs.The annualized funding rate acts as a highly effective, market-driven proxy for the cost of carry. A positive funding rate implies a positive cost of carry, analogous to a negative dividend yield in traditional equity formulas. The theoretical forward price for a crypto asset can be defined as:
$$F = S \exp((r_{crypto} - r_{usd})T)$$However, directly observing and isolating $r_{crypto}$ and $r_{usd}$ is often noisy. Instead, quantitative practitioners extract the implied forward price directly from the option chain itself, relying on the fundamental law of one price.4.3 Derivation of Put-Call Parity for Inverse OptionsTo extract the forward price without relying on external interest rate curves, put-call parity (PCP) is utilized. For inverse options, the PCP relationship must be rigorously adjusted to account for the coin-margined nature of the contracts. According to the foundational literature on inverse options, the parity relationship is governed by the asset discount factor $D_a$ and the quote discount factor $D_q$.The strict pricing identity for inverse options is expressed as:$$c - p = D_a \left(1 - \frac{K}{F}\right)$$
Where $c$ and $p$ represent the observed market prices of the inverse call and put (denominated in the underlying cryptocurrency), $K$ is the strike price, $F$ is the implied forward price of the asset, and $D_a$ is the discount factor associated with the cryptocurrency asset.4.4 Empirical Extraction of the Implied Forward CurveThe beauty of the inverse put-call parity formula lies in its linear relationship with respect to the strike price $K$. By distributing the $D_a$ term, the equation can be rearranged into a standard slope-intercept form:$$c - p = D_a - \left(\frac{D_a}{F}\right) K$$By aggregating synchronous bid-ask midpoints for calls and puts at a specific maturity, quantitative analysts perform a linear Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression of the observed premium difference $(c - p)$ on the Y-axis against the strike $K$ on the X-axis. This regression allows for the simultaneous identification of both the discount factor and the forward price:Intercept ($\alpha$): Directly yields the asset discount factor $D_a$.Slope ($\beta$): Yields the negative ratio $-\frac{D_a}{F}$.The implied forward price $F$ for that specific maturity is then smoothly and unambiguously extracted as:$$F = -\frac{\alpha}{\beta}$$This model-free extraction methodology is absolute critical for the calibration pipeline. Relying on a noisy spot price or an inaccurate fiat interest rate curve will distort the calculation of the option's log-moneyness, defined as $k = \log(K/F)$. A distorted log-moneyness coordinate will subsequently destabilize the Rough Heston parameter space, leading the neural network to output highly erratic estimates for the mean reversion and variance parameters.5. Rough Volatility Regimes in Cryptocurrency MarketsThe foundational assumption of the Black-Scholes model—and to a large extent, the classical Heston model—is that volatility is either constant or driven by a standard Brownian motion process exhibiting independent increments. However, extensive empirical evidence across various asset classes demonstrates that volatility time series possess long-memory properties and exhibit sample paths that are significantly rougher than a standard random walk. This behavior is modeled mathematically using fractional Brownian motion characterized by a Hurst exponent $H \in (0, 0.5)$.5.1 Empirical Estimation of the Hurst ExponentIn traditional equity markets, such as the S&P 500 index, the pioneering work by Gatheral, Jaisson, and Rosenbaum (2018) established that volatility is rough, with the Hurst exponent widely estimated to be $H \approx 0.1$. For cryptocurrency markets, the academic literature indicates comparable, if not slightly rougher, volatility dynamics.Takaishi (2020) conducted rigorous empirical tests on Bitcoin log-volatility increments using Multifractal Detrended Fluctuation Analysis (MF-DFA). The findings decisively rejected the standard Brownian motion assumption, establishing that the Hurst exponent for Bitcoin is significantly less than 0.5. Specifically, the estimates placed $H$ in the range of $0.09$ to $0.14$, indicating extreme anti-persistence and rough log-volatility increments. Similarly, empirical studies analyzing the fractal dimensions of Ethereum have confirmed parallel behavior, demonstrating that ETH realized volatility series also exhibit profound roughness.5.2 Market Events, Structural Breaks, and HalvingsCrucially, the Hurst exponent in cryptocurrency markets is not perfectly static. The generalized Hurst exponent is a function of multifractality, and its value fluctuates depending on the structural integrity of the market. During periods of severe market dislocation—such as major exchange liquidations (e.g., the collapse of FTX), aggressive regulatory interventions, or macroeconomic liquidity shocks—the serial dependence of volatility shifts violently.Furthermore, programmed supply reductions known as Bitcoin halving events operate as distinct structural breaks. Analyses utilizing Bai-Perron breakpoint tests indicate that halving events briefly alter the interoperability and memory dynamics of the BTC time series, often leading to a temporary normalization of the Hurst exponent closer to 0.5 before settling back into a rough regime. Consequently, setting $H$ as a learnable parameter during the FNO calibration process, rather than treating it as a fixed universal constant, is vital for maintaining pricing accuracy across turbulent and shifting market regimes.5.3 Realized Volatility Estimation: The Yang-Zhang OHLCV FormulationTo historically backtest rough volatility properties and establish ground-truth metrics for model initialization, the precise calculation of realized volatility is required. Simple close-to-close estimators, which rely solely on daily closing prices, severely underestimate intraday variance and are wholly inadequate for a highly volatile 24/7 market.The Yang-Zhang volatility estimator provides the optimal solution. It is recognized as the most powerful volatility estimator, featuring minimum estimation error while being entirely independent of drift and resilient to opening gaps. It optimally combines overnight volatility, open-to-close volatility, and the Rogers-Satchell estimator to account for intraday path dependencies using 1-minute OHLCV (Open, High, Low, Close, Volume) data.The mathematical formulation for the Yang-Zhang variance is:$$\sigma_{YZ}^2 = \sigma_{OC}^2 + k \sigma_{RS}^2$$Where the Rogers-Satchell variance component, which specifically accounts for intra-day high/low price movements, is defined over $N$ periods as:$$\sigma_{RS}^2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( \ln\left(\frac{H_i}{C_i}\right) \ln\left(\frac{H_i}{O_i}\right) + \ln\left(\frac{L_i}{C_i}\right) \ln\left(\frac{L_i}{O_i}\right) \right)$$The weighting factor $k$ is typically optimized empirically by Yang and Zhang to a value of $0.34$. By querying the Deribit public/get_tradingview_chart_data API endpoint to pull 1-minute bars, quantitative systems can continuously compute the Yang-Zhang metric. This serves as an exceptionally accurate proxy for the instantaneous variance $V_0$, providing a robust anchoring point for the initial state of the Rough Heston model.6. Numerical Instabilities and Calibration Challenges in the Rough Heston ModelThe Rough Heston model defines the variance process $V_t$ as a stochastic Volterra equation, integrating the fractional kernel:$$V_t = V_0 + \frac{1}{\Gamma(H + 0.5)} \int_{0}^{t} (t-s)^{H-0.5} \kappa (\theta - V_s) ds + \frac{\sigma}{\Gamma(H + 0.5)} \int_{0}^{t} (t-s)^{H-0.5} \sqrt{V_s} dW_s$$While this fractional representation perfectly captures the steep, short-dated volatility skew observed in cryptocurrency markets, it inherently strips the model of the Markovian and semimartingale properties that define the classical Heston model. This loss of mathematical tractability introduces formidable challenges for numerical pricing and calibration.6.1 The Fractional Riccati Equation and Short-Maturity BehaviorPricing vanilla options within the Fourier inversion framework requires determining the characteristic function of the log-price. In the classical Heston model, this function possesses an elegant closed-form solution. However, in the Rough Heston model, the characteristic function $\phi(u)$ cannot be resolved analytically. Instead, it is expressed in terms of the solution to a deterministic fractional Riccati equation:$$D^\alpha h(x, u) = \frac{1}{2} (-u^2 - iu) + \dots$$
Where $D^\alpha$ denotes the fractional derivative operator of order $\alpha = H + 0.5$. The absence of an explicit form dictates that the characteristic function must be approximated numerically.6.2 Failure of the COS Method for High Volatility and Small TThe standard numerical approach for evaluating the characteristic integral and pricing options is the Fourier-cosine series expansion, widely known as the COS method. However, deploying the standard COS method for cryptocurrency options introduces severe numerical instability, specifically when the time to maturity is very small ($T < 0.1$ years, which captures the daily and weekly expiries that dominate Deribit trading volumes).Under the Rough Heston framework, particularly with the extreme volatility of volatility ($\sigma$) parameters required for Bitcoin and Ethereum, the characteristic function exhibits highly oscillatory behavior and an agonizingly slow rate of decay in the tails. The COS method relies on truncating the integration domain; when applied to these slow-decaying, high-frequency oscillations, the truncation fails to capture critical tail probabilities. This truncation error manifests catastrophically as explosive oscillations in the implied volatility skew, and in extreme cases, produces mathematically impossible negative option prices for deep out-of-the-money strikes.6.3 Rational Padé Approximations and the Lifted Heston FrameworkTo bypass the paralyzing instability and extreme computational cost of traditional fractional integration schemes (such as the fractional Adams-Bashforth-Moulton method), quantitative researchers have pioneered two dominant methodologies that enable fast calibration:Rational (Padé) Approximations: Research by Gatheral and Radoičić demonstrated that the solution to the fractional Riccati equation can be tightly approximated by pasting together short-time and long-time mathematical expansions utilizing a rational Padé approximation, often referred to as Bernstein lifting. This analytical shortcut executes exponentially faster than fractional integration schemes while maintaining pricing accuracy even in the high-volatility regimes associated with crypto assets.The Lifted Heston Model: To fully resolve the non-Markovian difficulty, Abi Jaber introduced the Lifted Heston model. This structural framework approximates the fractional Volterra kernel using a finite sum of $N$ exponential kernels, essentially transforming the process into a multi-factor mean-reverting system. By systematically increasing the number of factors $N$, the Lifted Heston model converges uniformly to the theoretical Rough Heston model while simultaneously restoring Markovian properties. This restoration allows for the rapid evaluation of the characteristic function via standard, highly stable Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) solvers, paving the way for efficient deep learning data generation.7. Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) Deployment and Parameter Range ExtensionCalibration of complex stochastic volatility models is traditionally formulated as an inverse optimization problem, aiming to minimize the distance between model prices and market prices. For the Rough Heston model, iterative optimization routines are prohibitively slow. Fourier Neural Operators (FNO) provide a revolutionary mechanism to learn the complex, non-linear mapping from the option model parameters directly to the implied volatility surface, bypassing the evaluation of the characteristic function entirely during real-time inference. This neural network architecture accelerates the calibration process by several orders of magnitude, enabling true real-time parameter estimation.7.1 Boundary Constraints and Parameter Space ExpansionStandard FNO models developed in quantitative literature are overwhelmingly trained on equity parameter ranges, utilizing data from the S&P 500. The SPX historically features an annual volatility between 15% and 20%, leading to an initial variance range of $V_0 \in [0.01, 0.25]$.Cryptocurrencies exist in a vastly different parameter regime. Bitcoin routinely experiences annual volatilities of 60% to 80%, and Ethereum often exceeds 80% during bull cycles. High-Frequency Data Engineering and Storage PipelinesThe success of deep learning calibration models is fundamentally dependent on the quality, structure, and temporal resolution of the underlying data pipeline.8.1 Programmatic Snapshotting and Temporal ResolutionThe frequency of surface snapshots depends on the overarching calibration objective. For training and continuously updating a crypto-specific FNO, minute-level snapshots are highly recommended. This high temporal resolution provides the necessary volume of training data and captures the high-frequency regime shifts, localized volatility explosions, and mean-reverting behavior inherent to the crypto market.Utilizing the Deribit WebSocket ticker channels allows for the continuous aggregation of top-of-book state without violating REST rate limits. A background asynchronous process should aggregate these messages and construct a coherent order book snapshot at the turn of every minute.8.2 Storage Formats: Parquet vs. HDF5The resulting data structure from a continuous snapshotting process is highly dimensional, encompassing time, expiration, strike, bid, ask, and mark fields. Storing this multidimensional data in flat CSV files is highly inefficient, leading to massive storage overhead and severe bottlenecks during read operations.The Apache Parquet format is the strongly recommended storage standard for the data engineering pipeline. Parquet's columnar nature compresses option chain data with exceptional efficiency. Furthermore, it allows for predicate pushdown, enabling quantitative researchers to rapidly query specific maturities or strike ranges without loading the entire dataset into memory. Alternatively, HDF5 is suitable for managing the dense, multidimensional tensors that are directly ingested by PyTorch during the FNO training phase.8.3 Historical Reconstruction and Third-Party ProvidersFor historical data reconstruction—which is essential for bootstrapping the FNO training set before the live data collection pipeline has accrued sufficient history—relying solely on the Deribit public API is inadequate due to pagination limits.Specialized historical data providers, such as Tardis.dev, offer high-granularity, normalized historical tick and order book data for Deribit. Utilizing these third-party archives allows researchers to accurately reconstruct historical implied volatility surfaces dating back multiple years, ensuring the neural network is exposed to a wide variety of market regimes, including historical bull runs, crashes, and halving events.9.     async def fetch_endpoint(self, session, endpoint, params, retry_count=0):
        """Asynchronous HTTP GET to Deribit API with exponential backoff for 429 limits."""
        url = self.base_url + endpoint

                response.raise_for_status()
                data = await response.json()
                return data.get("result",)
        except Exception as e:
            logging.error(f"Failed to fetch {endpoint}: {str(e)}")
            return

        """Fetch the entire volatility surface snapshot in a single highly-efficient call."""

            puts = group[group['option_type'] == 'P'][['strike_price', 'mark_price']].set_index('strike_price')
            
            # Inner join to find paired strikes for the regression
            pairs = calls.join(puts, lsuffix='_call', rsuffix='_put').dropna()
            
            if len(pairs) < 3:
                logging.debug(f"Insufficient paired strikes for PCP extraction at {expiry}")
                continue
                
            # Dependent variable: c - p
            c_minus_p = pairs['mark_price_call'] - pairs['mark_price_put']
            # Independent variable: Strike Price K
            strikes = pairs.index.values
            
            # Perform Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) Linear Regression
            slope, intercept, r_value, p_value, std_err = linregress(strikes, c_minus_p)
            
            # F = - intercept / slope
            if slope!= 0 and intercept > 0:
                implied_forward = -intercept / slope
                forwards[expiry] = {
                    'implied_forward': implied_forward,
                    'asset_discount_factor': intercept,
                    'r_squared': r_value**2
                }
                
        return forwards

    async def construct_calibration_grid(self):
        """Main execution sequence to build the cleaned, calibrated surface grid."""
        async with aiohttp.ClientSession() as session:
            summary = await self.get_book_summary(session)
            
        if not summary:
            return pd.DataFrame()

        df = pd.DataFrame(summary)
        df = self.parse_instrument_name(df)
        
        # Microstructural Filter: Retain only liquid contracts with Open Interest > 0
        df = df[df['open_interest'] > 0].copy()
        
        # Calculate precise time to maturity (tau) in years
        current_time = datetime.utcnow()
        df['tau'] = (df['expiry_date'] - current_time).dt.total_seconds() / (365.25 * 24 * 3600)
        
        # Filter out severely short-dated options that may cause numerical instability
        df = df[df['tau'] > 0.002] 
        
        # Extract implied forwards via Put-Call Parity
        forwards = self.extract_forward_via_pcp(df)
        
        # Map the extracted forwards back to the DataFrame
        df['implied_forward'] = df['expiry_date'].map(lambda x: forwards.get(x, {}).get('implied_forward', np.nan))
        df = df.dropna(subset=['implied_forward'])
        
        # Compute exact log-moneyness for the FNO grid
        df['log_moneyness'] = np.log(df['strike_price'] / df['implied_forward'])
        
        # Convert Deribit's internal Black-Scholes mark_iv from percentage to decimal
        df['implied_volatility'] = df['mark_iv'] / 100.0 
        
        logging.info(f"Successfully constructed calibration grid with {len(df)} active nodes.")
        
        return df[['tau', 'log_moneyness', 'implied_volatility', 'strike_price', 'option_type']]
