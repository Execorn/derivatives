Advanced Calibration of the Rough Heston Model to SPX Options via Fourier Neural Operator SurrogatesThe transition from classical stochastic volatility modeling to the rough volatility paradigm represents a structural shift in quantitative finance. While the classical Heston model provides significant analytical tractability via its affine structure and Fourier pricing techniques, it systematically fails to capture the extreme explosion of the at-the-money (ATM) implied volatility skew for short maturities. This power-law behavior of the ATM skew as time-to-maturity approaches zero is a well-documented empirical feature of the S&P 500 (SPX) options market. The rough Heston model uniquely resolves this empirical discrepancy by driving the variance process with a fractional Brownian motion exhibiting a Hurst exponent $H < 1/2$.However, the introduction of a fractional integral kernel breaks the Markovianity and semimartingale properties of the underlying processes. Consequently, the characteristic function of the rough Heston model is no longer determined by a system of ordinary differential equations (ODEs) but rather by a fractional Riccati equation. Solving this equation using multi-point Padé approximants or Adams-Bashforth-Moulton fractional solvers is computationally prohibitive, taking milliseconds per evaluation. When embedded within an optimization loop requiring tens of thousands of evaluations, live market calibration becomes unfeasible.To overcome this fundamental bottleneck, quantitative desks have increasingly turned to Operator Learning. Specifically, the Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) serves as an ultra-fast, resolution-invariant pricing surrogate, mapping the infinite-dimensional rough Heston parameters directly to the implied volatility surface in microseconds. This report provides an exhaustive, end-to-end technical guide to calibrating the rough Heston model to real SPX market data using an FNO surrogate. It covers rigorous data acquisition protocols, meticulous preprocessing pipelines, static arbitrage removal, model parametrization across distinct market regimes, and the modern Python technology stack required for robust, production-grade execution.1. Data Cleaning and Preprocessing PipelineRaw options data is intrinsically noisy, populated with stale quotes, artificially wide spreads, and arbitrage violations. Calibrating an advanced stochastic volatility model like the rough Heston model on unfiltered data guarantees algorithmic non-convergence, the generation of "ghost calibrations," and severely distorted parameter estimates. The preprocessing pipeline is a strict sequence of liquidity filtering, yield extraction, and arbitrage enforcement.2.1 Filtering Illiquid and Unreliable OptionsTo ensure the calibration algorithm isolates genuine market information rather than market-maker quote noise, a series of liquidity heuristics is applied. The goal is to isolate the "active" volatility surface.Minimum Open Interest: Contracts with an openInterest below a strict threshold (typically 50 or 100) are discarded. A lack of open interest suggests the quote is merely a placeholder by a designated market maker, not representing true price discovery.Strictly Positive Bids: Contracts where bid <= 0 are excluded. A zero bid typically occurs for deep out-of-the-money (OTM) options where the theoretical value falls below the minimum tick size. Including these distorts the wings of the volatility smile.Bid-Ask Spread Threshold: Quotes where the relative spread is excessively wide are excluded. The standard quantitative finance rule is to filter options where $(Ask - Bid) / Mid > 0.20$ (or 20%). In periods of extreme stress (e.g., the VIX spike of August 2024), this threshold may be relaxed to 30% to retain enough data points for the calibration to proceed.2.2 Implied Forwards, Risk-Free Rates, and Dividend YieldsTo map absolute strike prices to log-moneyness, one requires the forward price of the index, $F_T$. The SPX is a capitalization-weighted index that does not pay a discrete dividend directly; instead, the continuous stream of dividends from its 500 constituents is modeled as a continuous yield $q$. Furthermore, matching the correct risk-free rate $r$ to the option's maturity $T$ is notoriously difficult using Treasury yields due to funding spreads and the "convenience yield" of cash.To bypass external rate curves, practitioners extract $r$ and $q$ directly from the options market using the Put-Call Parity relationship for European options :
$$C(K, T) - P(K, T) = S_0 e^{-qT} - K e^{-rT}$$By rearranging this equation into a linear regression model for a fixed maturity $T$, we define:$$y_i = C(K_i, T) - P(K_i, T) \\ x_i = K_i \\ y_i = \text{slope} \cdot x_i + \text{intercept}$$Performing an Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression across highly liquid, near-the-money strikes yields :$\text{slope} = -e^{-rT} \implies r = -\frac{\ln(-\text{slope})}{T}$$\text{intercept} = S_0 e^{-qT} \implies q = -\frac{\ln(\text{intercept} / S_0)}{T}$The implied forward price $F_T$ is then calculated as:$$F_T = S_0 e^{(r - q)T} = -\frac{\text{intercept}}{\text{slope}}$$This technique accurately captures the implied repo rate and dividend point adjustment priced into the market.Pythonimport numpy as np
import pandas as pd
from scipy.stats import linregress

def compute_implied_rates_and_forwards(df, spot_price, T):
    """
    Extracts r, q, and F from aligned call and put data using Put-Call Parity.
    Expects df to contain ['strike', 'mid_c', 'mid_p'] for a specific maturity T.
    """
    # Filter for liquid near-the-money strikes to improve regression accuracy
    df_filtered = df[(df['strike'] > spot_price * 0.8) & (df['strike'] < spot_price * 1.2)]
    
    y = df_filtered['mid_c'] - df_filtered['mid_p']
    x = df_filtered['strike']
    
    # y = slope * x + intercept
    slope, intercept, _, _, _ = linregress(x, y)
    
    # Protect against anomalous regression results
    if slope >= 0 or intercept <= 0:
        return None, None, None
        
    r = -np.log(-slope) / T
    q = -np.log(intercept / spot_price) / T
    F = spot_price * np.exp((r - q) * T)
    
    return r, q, F

# Apply log-moneyness transformation
def compute_log_moneyness(df, F):
    df['log_moneyness'] = np.log(df['strike'] / F)
    return df
Handling SPY vs SPX: It is critical to note that the OLS Put-Call Parity regression is only strictly valid for European-style options like the SPX. If attempting to calibrate on SPY (the ETF tracking the S&P 500), the options are American-style. SPY puts carry an early exercise premium, which violates the strict equality of Put-Call Parity. To "de-Americanize" SPY options, one must compute the early exercise premium using a binomial tree or finite difference grid, subtract it from the market price, and then apply the regression to the residual "European" equivalent prices. For pure rough Heston calibration, sticking to SPX European options is universally preferred.2.3 Enforcing Static Arbitrage ConditionsBefore calibration, the option surface must be demonstrably free of static arbitrage. If the raw data violates these boundaries, it implies negative implied probabilities or negative forward variances, causing the neural operator mapping to fail or produce nonsensical output.The two primary static arbitrage conditions that must be checked and repaired (or filtered) are :Calendar Spread Arbitrage (Time Continuity): For any two maturities $T_1 < T_2$ and a fixed strike $K$, the price of a call option must strictly increase with time (assuming continuous dividend yields are properly discounted).$$C(K, T_1) \le C(K, T_2)$$
A violation here indicates a negative forward variance between $T_1$ and $T_2$. If $C(T_1) > C(T_2)$, a trader could buy the $T_2$ call, sell the $T_1$ call, and lock in a risk-free profit if the stock stays flat.Butterfly Arbitrage (Strike Convexity): Option prices must be strictly convex with respect to the strike price. For any three evenly spaced strikes $K_1 < K_2 < K_3$ where $\Delta K = K_2 - K_1 = K_3 - K_2$:$$C(K_1) - 2C(K_2) + C(K_3) \ge 0$$
The second derivative of the call price with respect to the strike is proportional to the risk-neutral probability density function of the underlying asset. A violation of convexity implies a negative probability density, a mathematical impossibility.To construct a robust dataset, algorithmic pipelines either drop the offending quotes or utilize linear programming "arbitrage repair" algorithms to perturb the quotes within their bid-ask bounds until the constraints are satisfied. For most FNO surrogate training and inference, dropping the anomalous points is sufficient if the market is deep.Pythondef check_butterfly_arbitrage(df_slice):
    """
    Checks for strict convexity across strikes for a single expiration slice.
    Expects df_slice to be sorted by strike.
    """
    df_slice = df_slice.sort_values('strike').reset_index(drop=True)
    prices = df_slice['mid'].values
    strikes = df_slice['strike'].values
    
    valid_indices =  # Keep the first point
    
    for i in range(1, len(prices) - 1):
        # Using discrete second derivative approximation for non-uniform grids
        w1 = (strikes[i+1] - strikes[i]) / (strikes[i+1] - strikes[i-1])
        w3 = (strikes[i] - strikes[i-1]) / (strikes[i+1] - strikes[i-1])
        
        convexity = w1 * prices[i-1] + w3 * prices[i+1] - prices[i]
        
        if convexity >= 0:
            valid_indices.append(i)
            
    valid_indices.append(len(prices) - 1) # Keep the last point
    return df_slice.iloc[valid_indices]
3. Implied Volatility Surface ConstructionThe raw output of the data cleaning pipeline is a set of arbitrage-free option prices. Because the impliedVolatility field from free data vendors is frequently calculated using lagging historical spot prices, quantitative workflows strictly demand the recomputation of Black-Scholes implied volatilities (BS IV) directly from the synchronous NBBO mid-prices.3.1 Numerical Inversion via py_vollib and QuantLibThe relationship between a European option price and implied volatility is given by the Black-Scholes formula. Because volatility cannot be isolated algebraically, it requires numerical root-finding. Standard algorithms like Newton-Raphson or Brent's method often fail for deep OTM or deep ITM options, or as $T \to 0$. In these regions, Vega (the derivative of the option price with respect to volatility) approaches zero, causing the Newton step to diverge or precision to vanish.To handle these extreme limits robustly across millions of data rows, practitioners utilize Peter Jäckel’s "Let's Be Rational" (LBR) algorithm, which relies on rational approximations to guarantee analytical convergence to full machine precision for all arbitrage-free option prices.In the Python ecosystem, this algorithm is historically accessed via the py_vollib library.Pythonfrom vollib.black_scholes.implied_volatility import implied_volatility
import numpy as np

def compute_iv_vectorized(prices, strikes, S, t, r, flag='c'):
    """
    Computes IV using Peter Jäckel's Let's Be Rational algorithm.
    """
    ivs = np.zeros(len(prices))
    for i, (p, K) in enumerate(zip(prices, strikes)):
        try:
            # LBR guarantees convergence if price is arbitrage-free
            ivs[i] = implied_volatility(p, S, K, t, r, flag)
        except Exception:
            ivs[i] = np.nan
    return ivs

While highly robust for pricing complex exotics, QuantLib's IV inversion can be marginally slower than vollib's LBR implementation for massive, batched DataFrame operations.3.2 Interpolation and Extrapolation via SVI ParameterizationOptions market quotes fall on discrete, irregular grids dictated by exchange listing rules (e.g., strikes at 5-point or 25-point intervals). However, Operator learning models like the Fourier Neural Operator perform optimally when trained and evaluated on continuous functions or dense, highly regular spatial grids. Therefore, the discrete IVs must be parameterized and interpolated.The industry standard for modeling individual expiration slices is the Stochastic Volatility Inspired (SVI) parameterization, introduced by Jim Gatheral in 2004. The raw SVI formulation represents total implied variance $w(k) = \sigma_{IV}^2 T$ as a hyperbola in log-moneyness $k$:$$w(k) = a + b \left( \rho(k - m) + \sqrt{(k - m)^2 + \sigma^2} \right)$$The five parameters carry distinct geometric meanings :$a$: Overall level of variance (vertical translation).$b$: Slope of the wings (convexity or butterfly effect).$\rho$: Correlation parameter dictating the skew or asymmetry of the smile. For SPX, $\rho$ is highly negative.$m$: Translates the minimum of the curve horizontally.$\sigma$: Controls the tightness or curvature at the ATM point.To ensure the interpolated curve does not introduce butterfly arbitrage, SVI calibration must satisfy the Durrleman condition. This requires constrained non-linear optimization, typically implemented via scipy.optimize.minimize using the L-BFGS-B or Differential Evolution algorithms.A more financially intuitive formulation used by trading desks is the SVI Jump-Wings (SVI-JW) parameterization, which re-expresses the raw SVI parameters in terms of observable quantities: ATM variance $v_t$, ATM skew $\psi_t$, put wing slope $p_t$, call wing slope $c_t$, and minimum implied variance $\tilde{v}_t$. Calibrating the Jump-Wings parameters directly prevents the optimizer from wandering into unphysical parameter spaces.Plotting raw market implied volatilities as discrete scatter points against the parameterized continuous SVI curve and the FNO predicted curve demonstrates the precise alignment between empirical data and theoretical modeling. The SVI curve bridges the gap between noisy market data and the smooth, continuous inputs demanded by neural operator architectures, ensuring that the FNO trains on high-fidelity representations of market skew rather than fitting the noise of bid-ask bounce.For maturity selection during the calibration pipeline, standard procedure targets the most liquid tenors: 1W, 2W, 1M, 2M, 3M, 6M, 9M, 1Y, and 2Y. Capturing the extreme convexity of short-dated weekly options (0DTE to 1W) is particularly critical, as this is the exact regime where classical models fail and the rough Heston model asserts its superiority.4. Model Calibration Specifics: The Rough Heston FrameworkThe classical Heston model describes instantaneous variance $V_t$ as a mean-reverting square-root process driven by a standard Brownian motion. While mathematically elegant, its Markovian nature prevents it from accurately reproducing the term structure of the volatility skew observed in equity markets.The quadratic rough Heston model solves this by replacing the standard Brownian motion in the variance process with a fractional process. This fractional integration is governed by a kernel modeled on the Mittag-Leffler function, mapping microstructural Hawkes processes (representing the persistence of order flow and the Zumbach effect of price-trend feedback) to macroscopic rough volatility.The dynamics of the rough Heston model under the risk-neutral measure are expressed as:$$dS_t = S_t \sqrt{V_t} dW_t$$$$V_t = V_0 + \frac{1}{\Gamma(H + 1/2)} \int_0^t (t-s)^{H - 1/2} \lambda (\theta - V_s) ds + \frac{\nu}{\Gamma(H + 1/2)} \int_0^t (t-s)^{H - 1/2} \sqrt{V_s} dZ_s$$
where $dW_t$ and $dZ_t$ are Brownian motions with a constant correlation $\rho$.4.1 Parameterization and the Role of the Hurst Exponent ($H$)Calibrating the model requires optimizing the parameter vector $\Theta = \{H, \nu, \rho, V_0, \theta, \lambda\}$ to match the market implied volatility surface:$H$ (Hurst Exponent): Measures the roughness of the volatility paths. A value of $H = 1/2$ recovers the standard Heston model. Values $H < 1/2$ indicate anti-persistent, rough trajectories.$\nu$ (Vol-of-Vol): Dictates the magnitude of variance fluctuations. (Often denoted as $\sigma$ or $\xi$ in classical literature, but $\nu$ distinguishes it in the fractional context).$\rho$ (Correlation): Captures the leverage effect. For the SPX, this is strictly negative, as market drops correlate with volatility spikes.$V_0$: The initial instantaneous variance level.$\theta$: The long-term mean to which the variance process reverts.$\lambda$ (Mean Reversion Speed): The rate at which variance returns to $\theta$. (Often denoted as $\kappa$ in classical Heston literature).A critical operational decision is how to handle the Hurst exponent $H$. Seminal empirical literature by Gatheral et al. (2018) demonstrated that the historical roughness of equity volatility consistently centers around $H \approx 0.05$ to $0.1$. During joint calibration across the entire parameter space, the optimization landscape with respect to $H$ is notoriously flat. This creates severe identifiability issues where varying $H$ is artificially compensated by scaling $\nu$, leading to non-unique solutions. Consequently, to ensure stable day-over-day calibration, quantitative practitioners frequently fix $H$ (e.g., $H = 0.05$ or $H=0.1$) and calibrate the remaining five parameters.4.2 Loss Function DesignThe objective of calibration is to find the parameter set $\Theta^*$ that minimizes the distance between the market-implied volatility and the model-implied volatility. Using a naive unweighted Mean Squared Error (MSE) biases the calibration toward deep out-of-the-money options that have little financial significance but large raw volatility numbers.Practitioners deploy weighted loss functions to prioritize financially relevant strikes:$$L(\Theta) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \omega_i \left^2$$The weights $\omega_i$ are engineered based on two primary philosophies:Inverse Bid-Ask Spread: $\omega_i = (\text{Ask}_i - \text{Bid}_i)^{-1}$. This forces the optimizer to fit highly liquid instruments (which have tight spreads) with extreme precision, while allowing slack for illiquid wings where price discovery is poor.Vega-Weighted: $\omega_i = \mathcal{V}_i^2$. Because a pricing error is approximately the product of the volatility error and Vega ($\Delta \text{Price} \approx \mathcal{V} \cdot \Delta \sigma$), weighting by the square of Vega ensures the model accurately prices the options carrying the most absolute dollar risk in a portfolio.4.3 FNO Surrogate Model IntegrationTraditional calibration loops require evaluating the loss function thousands of times. For the rough Heston model, each evaluation requires computing the characteristic function via the solution to a fractional Riccati equation in the complex plane. This numerical integration takes milliseconds per quote, amounting to minutes per surface—a latency intolerable for live trading systems or historical backtesting.The Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) entirely resolves this computational bottleneck. Trained offline on millions of synthetically generated parameter combinations $\{\Theta, k, T\}$, the FNO learns the infinite-dimensional, non-linear mapping between the parametric inputs and the continuous IV surface.The FNO achieves this via a branch-trunk architecture that maps parameters into the Fourier domain. By parameterizing integral kernels in Fourier space, it captures long-range correlations across the surface and remains resolution-invariant. Once trained, a single forward pass of the FNO evaluates the entire volatility surface in microseconds. In the calibration loop, scipy.optimize.minimize iteratively updates $\Theta$, passes it through the PyTorch FNO model, retrieves the predicted $\sigma_{IV}^{FNO}$ tensor, and calculates the weighted loss until convergence is achieved.4.4 Regime Analysis and Parameter BehaviorThe calibrated parameters of the rough Heston model provide deep insights into market dynamics, varying violently depending on the prevailing macroeconomic regime.Normal Environments (e.g., June 18, 2021): During low-volatility, steady-growth periods, the parameters reflect stable dynamics. The initial variance $V_0$ is low, the volatility of volatility ($\nu$) remains moderate, and the correlation ($\rho$) is solidly negative but not extreme. Mean reversion ($\lambda$) operates at standard market paces.Stress Regimes (e.g., September 12, 2008 & March 16, 2020): During massive dislocation events where the VIX spikes above 60 to 80 (as seen during the COVID-19 crash or the Lehman Brothers collapse) , the short-dated ATM skew steepens astronomically. Calibration exhibits an explosion in $\nu$, reflecting severe market uncertainty, and an even stronger negative correlation $\rho$ to model the extreme, jump-like downward trajectories of the SPX coincident with massive volatility spikes. Validation and DiagnosticsPost-calibration, validating the FNO surrogate model requires rigorous statistical analysis. The primary risk in deep learning calibration is "ghost calibration"—where numerical errors inherent in the FNO's approximation offset underlying model specification errors, yielding an apparently perfect fit to market prices that completely unravels when computing Greeks for hedging.5.1 Residual Analysis and RMSEThe primary diagnostic tool is the heat map of residual volatility points across the $(T, k)$ grid:$$\text{Residual}(K, T) = \sigma_{IV}^{Mkt}(K, T) - \sigma_{IV}^{FNO}(K, T)$$A highly successful, well-calibrated rough Heston model on the SPX achieves a typical Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) of roughly 0.5 to 2.0 volatility points (0.005 to 0.020 in decimal format) across the liquid segments of the surface.Interpreting the residuals requires distinguishing between two sources of error:Model Misspecification: Identified by systematic spatial patterns. For example, if the model consistently underprices deep OTM puts (yielding a swath of negative skew residuals), it suggests the correlation $\rho$ constraint is hitting an artificial lower bound, or that the market is pricing in discrete jump components that continuous rough volatility cannot fully capture.Data Quality Issues: Indicated by erratic, isolated spikes in the residual map. These generally correspond to stale quotes, microstructural noise, or wide bid-ask spreads that bypassed the initial filtering algorithm.5.2 Comparative Superiority vs. Classical ModelsDiagnostics uniformly highlight the superiority of the rough Heston framework over classical methods when validated on out-of-sample strikes.Standard Heston: The classical Markovian Heston model systematically fails the "term structure of skew" test. It cannot simultaneously fit the extreme steepness of weekly SPX option skews while matching the flatter shape of 1-year maturities without resorting to piecewise-constant, time-dependent parameters that break time-homogeneity. Rough volatility solves this parsimoniously, generating accurate power-law behavior in the short-dated skew automatically via the fractional kernel.SABR: While the SABR model handles ATM skew well, the rough Heston model provides superior control over the wings. SABR was fundamentally designed for interest rate markets and struggles with the severe leverage effects and deep negative correlation typical of broad equity indices like the SPX.5.3 Parameter StabilityA robust model must exhibit parameter stability; the calibrated parameters should not oscillate wildly from day to day absent significant macroeconomic news. A properly supervised FNO surrogate—trained with physics-informed penalties or conformal bootstrap principles—ensures that the optimization landscape remains smooth and convex enough to prevent the optimizer from becoming trapped in local minima. A daily shift of $\Delta \nu > 50\%$ on a quiet trading day indicates algorithmic instability. Utilizing Lipschitz projections, enforcing strict parameter bounds, and fixing the Hurst exponent $H$ are standard techniques to mitigate erratic parameter jumping and ensure temporal continuity across trading days.6. import os

def cache_options_data(df, ticker, date_str, cache_dir="./data_cache"):
    """
    Saves the DataFrame to a compressed Parquet file, preserving datatypes.
    """

    df.to_parquet(file_path, engine='pyarrow', compression='snappy')
    print(f"Data successfully cached to {file_path}")

    """
    Loads options data from the Parquet cache.
    """
